Cordless Drill Buying Guide

Brushless vs Brushed Motors

Brushless motors run cooler, last longer, and are more efficient than brushed motors — meaning more runtime per charge and a longer tool life. They cost more upfront, but for anything beyond light occasional use, they're worth it. Every pick in our top 3 uses a brushless motor.

Voltage: 12V vs 18V vs 20V

For most homeowners, 18V or 20V (they're essentially the same — just marketing) is the sweet spot. It gives you plenty of power for 90% of tasks without the weight of a larger tool. 12V drills are lighter but limited. Anything above 20V is for specialized applications.

Battery Capacity (Ah)

The amp-hour (Ah) rating tells you how long a battery lasts. A 2.0Ah battery gives you less runtime than a 5.0Ah battery. For light use, 2.0Ah is fine. If you're doing projects all day, get at least 4.0Ah batteries.

Chuck Size

Most full-size drills come with a ½" chuck, which accepts larger bits. Budget drills often have 3/8" chucks. For home use, either works — but ½" gives you more flexibility.

Frequently Asked Questions

What's the best cordless drill for a beginner?

The BLACK+DECKER 20V MAX is ideal for beginners — it's affordable, easy to use, and comes with everything you need. If you want something that'll last longer and handle more jobs, step up to the DeWalt DCD791D2.

Is DeWalt or Milwaukee better?

Both are excellent. DeWalt has a slight edge in ergonomics and the 3-speed transmission. Milwaukee has better runtime and torque for the size. You can't go wrong with either — it often comes down to which ecosystem your other tools are in.

Do I need a drill and a driver, or just one?

For most homeowners, a drill/driver combo handles both tasks. A dedicated impact driver is better for driving lots of screws quickly, but it's not necessary unless you're doing a lot of that specific work.
